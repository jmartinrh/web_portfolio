DND - Balancing


Context
Our DND island offered players three routes to reach the final boss fight: easy, medium, and hard. Tougher route, higher reward. We had a simple system tying room and route difficulty to coins-per-enemy and type of biome.


Problem
The original system worked to deliver the rewards we wanted for player progression. However, as we expanded our enemy selection and included minibosses, we needed better balancing tools for the whole experience. Additionally, we had delivered encounters that players identified as frustrating and unrewarding.


Goal
Creating a new basis for our game’s balance so we could tweak rooms with data, not guts, and increase player retention.


Solution
The solution was multifaceted and required me to approach the problem from both a systems and level design perspective.


Alongside our Product Manager Jorge Castaño and User Researcher Nicolás Perez we reviewed game analytics and player feedback. We identified the rooms players found unfair: rooms with high death and quit ratios. 


Some rooms had a bad combination of layout and enemy weapons: Explosive weapons in Fortnite are powerful and can damage players behind cover. Layouts that limited cover rotation options or had strong enemy highground positions put players at a big disadvantage. Other rooms were too crowded. In some parts of the map, the difficulty spike between rooms was too steep and poorly communicated.


This prompted me to review level design elements that impacted difficulty: room layouts, exit signposting, enemy positions, and cover. I highlighted rooms that needed fixes and drafted an action plan.


At the same time I had discussions with my Design Lead César Solis to understand how we wanted to tie room difficulty and the economy together and started working on some formulas. I calculated each enemy’s individual Threat Value and then each room’s.


Defensive Threat Value
	(Total Health / 1000)^Defensive Coefficient
	Offensive Threat Value
	DPS * Accuracy Value * Weapon Type / Enemy Max DPS
	Individual Threat Value
	Defensive Value * Offensive Value
	Group Threat Value
	SUM(Individual Threat…)
	Room Threat Value
	LOG10((Threat * Threat Coefficient)*(Number of Enemies in Room ^ Crowd Exponent)) + Base Threat Coefficient
	

Graphing both Room Difficulty and gold-per-enemy values empowered us to clearly see the relationship between difficulty and reward and better understand why players were complaining. We identified priorities, strategies, and a roadmap that would quickly address player feedback.


* Adjusted enemy health, number, and weapon selection
* Adjusted enemy rewards
* Adjusted problematic enemy spawn locations and timing
* Increased cover and adjusted navigation in some rooms
* Added signposting that indicated increased challenge in exits that took players from one route to another
* Improved clarity of one-way and alternative room exits


Result
First, we now had a tool that at a glance helped us understand our games' difficulty and encounter progression. This also empowered our capacity to tweak difficulty when needed.


But more importantly, the changes had a big impact on player retention and difficulty perception.


Walking Dead Level Design
Context
For The Walking Dead experience, I had designed an interconnected map that had 3 mirrored team areas. Each team had to go through a PvE section before reaching the PvPvE arenas where the king-of-the hill dynamics happened. I wanted a mix of claustrophobic spaces to spotlight the zombies and bigger arenas that supported PvE fighting.


The Problem
The dark and grimy aesthetic of the assets, paired with an objective that changed locations from above ground to underground and back made navigation confusing for players. Internal playtesters were getting disoriented and had trouble keeping up with the king-of-the-hill. The mirrored nature of the map also confused players as to where their team bases were located once in the main arenas.


Goal
Add signposting and HUD information to help players find their way through the prison map.


Solution
There were several fronts I had to tackle. First I went over the playtest reports and identified the most critical areas where players got lost. I followed by walking the level and getting a wayfinding understanding of what the problems could be. Disorientation mainly happened because decoration was too busy, pathing was not clear, and lighting was not being used to direct player attention.


Knowing just an art and light pass was not enough, I reviewed competitive shooters like CS:GO, Valorant, Apex Legends and Epic’s own Ballistic game mode to bring over some additional good practices. I then consulted with our UI/UX lead Andrés Pisso to get his take on what HUD elements would best help players find their way.


With all the information in hand I made a plan and coordinated meetings to present to stakeholders and other project area leads to get buy-in and feedback. I coordinated the plan with production, checked for dependencies, and got started:


* Collaborated with the environmental art team to add team colours to structures, clear pathing, and use light to direct player attention to exits.
* Added graffiti decals to signpost exits, areas, and directionality.
* Created a system that turned some decals on or off depending on where the capture area was located.
* Added HUD beacons that clamped to screen to mark each capture area when active
* Added HUD objective messages.
* Added notifications for important state changes that needed player attention.


Result
I delivered a map with tools that directed navigation both through the environment itself (diegetically) and through HUD elements (non-diegetically). Through playtesting and launch, we did not receive complaints or issues regarding wayfinding or objective clarity.
